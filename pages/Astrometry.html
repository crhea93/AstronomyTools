LScalc.py

This program has a very simple goal: calculate the linear distance of an astronomical object given a redshift and angular view. To do this, I simply utilized the following set of equations to calculate the quantity of interest. Note that we have to correct for the acceleration and expansion of the universe. \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|} \hline Parameter & Brief \ Description \\ \hline l & Linear \ Distance \ in \ Kiloparsecs \\ \hline z & Redshift \\ \hline d_A(z) & Angular \ Distance \\ \hline \theta & Angular \ Seperation \ in \ arcseconds \\ \hline d_H & Hubble \ Distance \\ \hline c & Speed \ of \ Light \\ \hline H_0 & Hubble \ Constant \\ \hline d_C & Comoving \ Distance \\ \hline E(z) & Energy \ Function \\ \hline d_M(z) & Moving \ Distance \\ \hline \Omega_{rel} & Mass \ Density \ of \ Relativistic \ Particles \\ \hline \Omega_{mass} & Mass \ Density \ of \ Baryonic \ and \ NonBaryonic \ Particles \\ \hline \Omega_{\Lambda} & Mass \ Density \ of \ Dark \ Energy \\ \hline \end{array}

Our set of equations are as follows: $$l = d_A(z)*\theta$$ $$d_A(z) = \frac{d_M(z)}{1+z}$$ $$d_M(z) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \frac{d_H}{\sqrt{\Omega_k}}\sinh\Big({\sqrt{\Omega_k}\frac{d_C(z)}{d_H}}\Big) & \Omega_k > 0 \\ d_C(z) & \Omega_k = 0 \\ \frac{d_H}{\sqrt{|\Omega_k|}}\sin\Big({\sqrt{|\Omega_k|}\frac{d_C(z)}{d_H}}\Big) & \Omega_k < 0 \\ \end{array} \right. $$ $$ d_C(z) = d_H\int_{0}^{z} \frac{dz'}{E(z')} $$ $$ E(z) = \sqrt{\Omega_{rel}(1+z)^4+\Omega_{mass}(1+z)^3+\Omega_k(1+z)^2+\Omega_{\Lambda}} $$ $$ \Omega_k = 1-\Omega_{mass}-\Omega_{\Lambda} $$ $$ d_H = \frac{c}{H_0} $$
